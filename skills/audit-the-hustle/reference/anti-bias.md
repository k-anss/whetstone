# Anti-bias: four questions, every run, before the conclusion

Answer each in one line. Attach the answers to the audit output — the reader is entitled to see the check happened.

## 1. Am I treating a famous name as the default answer?

Head-of-distribution names dominate training data and search results. "Well-known" and "authentic" are different properties; fame raises the *stakes* of performance, not the odds against it. If the subject is famous, ask: did I demand the same evidence I would demand from an unknown?

## 2. Is this person building a product, or is the content the product?

Two different audits. If the main observable output is *content about making money* — courses, threads, paid communities teaching the method — then the revenue claims are marketing for the content business, and signal class 1 must be checked against the content business, not the alleged underlying product. Misclassifying this is the most common way an audit flatters its subject.

## 3. Am I beautifying this?

A compelling narrative is evidence of narrative skill, nothing else. Check: did any signal get marked "present" on the strength of the subject's own storytelling rather than an independent trace? If the story is what moved the number, move it back.

## 4. Which conclusion lines are checklist-triggered, and which are my own reasoning?

Label every line. Checklist-triggered lines inherit the reliability of the signal classes — they were designed against known fakes. Own-reasoning lines are inference, with model bias riding along. Both are allowed in the conclusion; presenting them at equal weight is not.

---

If any question changes the draft conclusion, change the conclusion *and say that the check changed it*. A self-check that never moves anything is decoration.
